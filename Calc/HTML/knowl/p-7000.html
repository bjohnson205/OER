In Section 8.2, we encountered infinite geometric series. For example, by writing

\begin{equation*} N = 0.1212121212 \cdots = \frac{12}{100} + \frac{12}{100} \cdot \frac{1}{100} + \frac{12}{100} \cdot \frac{1}{100^2} + \cdots \end{equation*}

as a geometric series, we found a way to write \(N\) as a single fraction: \(N = \frac{4}{33}\text{.}\) In this section, we explore other types of infinite sums. In Preview Activity 8.9 we see how one such sum is related to the famous number \(e\text{.}\)
